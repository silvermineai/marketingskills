# E-E-A-T Signal Reference

How to weave Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness throughout a blog post without creating a dedicated "E-E-A-T section."

## Experience (First-Hand Knowledge)

The strongest E-E-A-T signal. Google's quality raters look for evidence the author has actually done the thing they're writing about.

### Patterns to Use

**Anonymized observations:**
- "In our experience working with [type of customer], we consistently see..."
- "A pattern we notice across our clients is..."
- "We've helped various businesses navigate [challenge], and the most common mistake is..."

**Aggregated insights:**
- "After working on dozens of [type of project], the single biggest factor is..."
- "Across our portfolio, teams that [do X] see better results than those that [do Y]"

**Process descriptions:**
- "Our approach starts with [specific first step] because..."
- "When we audit a client's [system], the first thing we check is..."

### What to Avoid
- Fabricating specific client names or case studies
- Making up exact numbers ("we increased conversions by 347%")
- Claiming personal experience the company doesn't have
- Using vague experience claims without any specifics

## Expertise (Deep Knowledge)

Demonstrate that you understand the topic better than surface-level content.

### Patterns to Use

**Nuanced takes:**
- "Most guides will tell you to [common advice], but this misses [important nuance]"
- "The conventional wisdom is [X], and it's mostly right — except when [specific exception]"

**Technical depth:**
- Use industry-specific terminology naturally (don't over-explain basics)
- Reference specific frameworks, methodologies, or standards by name
- Explain the "why" behind recommendations, not just the "what"

**Addressing misconceptions:**
- "A common mistake is thinking [misconception]. In reality, [correct understanding] because [reason]"

## Authoritativeness (Credible Sources)

Back claims with evidence and demonstrate the company's standing in the field.

### Patterns to Use

**Citations:**
- Link to original research, studies, and reports
- Reference official documentation and standards
- Cite industry analysts and recognized experts

**Company authority signals:**
- Link to the company's case studies or resources page
- Reference the company's work in the space (without over-promoting)
- Mention relevant experience, certifications, or partnerships

**Data:**
- Include specific numbers with attribution
- Use tables for data comparisons
- Link to the data source

## Trustworthiness (Honest and Transparent)

### Patterns to Use

**Balanced viewpoints:**
- "This approach works well for [situation], but has limitations when [other situation]"
- "The upside is [benefit]. The tradeoff is [cost or limitation]"

**Appropriate hedging:**
- "In most cases" instead of absolute claims
- "Based on available data" when citing statistics
- "Results vary depending on [factors]"

**Transparency:**
- Acknowledge when something is the company's opinion vs. established fact
- Note when information might change or be context-dependent
- If the company sells something related, be upfront about it

### What to Avoid
- Absolute guarantees ("this will definitely work")
- Hiding limitations or downsides
- Presenting opinions as facts
- Dismissing alternative approaches without explanation

## Quick Checklist

Before submitting a draft, verify:

- [ ] At least 2 experience-based observations (anonymized or aggregated)
- [ ] At least 1 nuanced take or misconception addressed
- [ ] At least 3 citations to authoritative external sources
- [ ] At least 1 link to company resources/case studies
- [ ] At least 1 balanced viewpoint with tradeoffs acknowledged
- [ ] No absolute guarantees or unverifiable claims
- [ ] Technical terminology used naturally (not over-explained)
